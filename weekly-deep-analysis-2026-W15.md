## 3. Fitness–Mental Health Correlation

**The body told a consistent story this week that Jim only partially heard.**

Lower back pain appears on Saturday and persists through Friday — seven consecutive days. Jim attributes it variously to carrying things (Sat), walking (Sun), weights (Mon), swimming (Thu), and unclear causes (Fri). The rotating attribution suggests he's treating each day as a separate event rather than seeing a sustained inflammatory state. The back is not recovering between activities because the recovery conditions aren't being met: sleep is better (7.6h) but still below his stated 8h need, and there's no dedicated rest day in the week.

**Steps (13,322 avg) and exercise (272 min/day)** are high — notably higher than what would be expected during a "holiday" week. This is the motion-as-safety pattern operating at a physiological level: the body is being used for regulation, and it's working (mood is generally stable, no spiralling), but the cost is accumulating in the musculoskeletal system. The back pain is the invoice.

**HRV (34.2ms)** is marginally improved from recent weeks but remains in the low band. The correlation data shows sleep_hours vs HRV at r=0.72 (sparse but strong signal) and sleep_hours vs mood_score at r=0.94 (extremely strong, though only n=3). Even with sparse data, the direction is unambiguous: sleep is the single variable most predictive of both physiological regulation and subjective wellbeing. The 7.6h average this week, while the best in months, is still 25 minutes short of his stated minimum — and the back pain suggests even this improved sleep isn't sufficient for physical recovery under high activity loads.

**Resting HR (60.6 bpm)** is reasonable and stable, suggesting no acute physiological stress — the system is under chronic low-grade load rather than acute crisis.

**Intervention effectiveness data** is telling: Movement Reset Walk shows the only positive HRV delta (+5.7ms) among tracked interventions. Yoga Downshift shows +1.0ms. Every other intervention (grounding, physiological sigh, mindfulness) shows *negative* HRV deltas, suggesting they may help subjectively but don't move the physiological needle. This aligns with Jim's lived experience: walking and yoga work; mental techniques are useful for acute moments but don't restore baseline.

**Focus/productivity:** Context-switch data isn't available for this week, but the journal entries suggest low cognitive demand throughout (family activities, not work). The one work-related stressor (Chris contracts) was deferred rather than engaged. HRV vs context_switches (r=0.24, n=31) suggests higher HRV correlates with *more* switching — counterintuitively, this may mean that when Jim is well-regulated he can handle transitions, rather than that switching helps regulation.

**The week's fitness-mood story in one line:** High physical output sustained mood but prevented recovery, and the body is asking for rest more loudly than Jim is hearing.
